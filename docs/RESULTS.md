# Results & Evaluation Methodology

All numbers below are from a real `LGBMRanker` run (`lightgbm==4.6.0`), reproducible via `python run_pipeline.py` → `model_training/metrics.json`. The pipeline is fully seeded, so the ranker numbers below reproduce bit-for-bit on re-run.

## Methodology

- **Data**: synthetic, generated by `data_generation/` (see `docs/CHANGES.md` for how labels are generated and why the process is non-circular).
- **Split**: group-aware, by `cart_id`, 80/20, via `GroupShuffleSplit`. Zero `cart_id` overlap between train and test, verified by an assertion in `train_ranker.py` (not just claimed — the script halts if it's violated). Run: 10,234 train carts / 2,559 test carts, 373,686 total (cart, candidate) rows.
- **Model**: `LGBMRanker(objective="lambdarank", metric="ndcg", eval_at=[3])`.
- **Baselines (three, deliberately spanning a range of context):**
  1. **Random** — uniform random ranking (pure chance).
  2. **Popularity** — rank candidates by `candidate_popularity_score` alone. This is a single static number per item: **zero context** — identical regardless of meal_time, city, or what's in the cart.
  3. **Context-aware** — rank by each item's empirical "added next" rate within its `(meal_time, candidate_category)` bucket, estimated on **train only** (empirical-Bayes shrunk toward the global positive rate) and scored on the held-out test set. This adds **meal_time context** but still **ignores the cart contents entirely**. It sits between popularity and the full ranker, which is the whole point of including it (see "Why a third baseline" below).
- **Metrics**: Precision@3, Recall@3, NDCG@3, computed per cart then averaged — standard top-k ranking metrics. All four scorers are evaluated on the *same* held-out test set, so the comparison is apples-to-apples.

## Headline numbers (real LGBMRanker run)

| Metric | **Ranker** (full cart + city + meal_time) | Context-aware (meal_time + item, no cart) | Popularity (item only) | Random |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Precision@3 | **0.2280** | 0.2205 | 0.1353 | 0.1301 |
| Recall@3 | **0.1753** | 0.1708 | 0.1019 | 0.0983 |
| NDCG@3 | **0.2390** | 0.2315 | 0.1411 | 0.1365 |

Relative differences (from `metrics.json`):

| Comparison | Precision@3 | Recall@3 | NDCG@3 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Popularity **over random** | +4.0% | +3.7% | +3.4% |
| Context-aware **over random** | +69.5% | +73.7% | +69.6% |
| Ranker **over popularity** | +68.4% | +72.0% | +69.3% |
| Ranker **over context-aware** | +3.4% | +2.7% | +3.2% |

### Read the lift number and the baseline-strength number together — never in isolation

The "+68% over popularity" headline is real, but it is **mostly a statement about how little the popularity baseline knows, not about cart modelling.** The popularity baseline is only **~4% above random**, because it ignores meal_time entirely and the dominant learnable signal in this data is a hidden `(meal_time, candidate_category)` affinity. The moment you give a baseline *just* meal_time context — no cart at all — it captures **~69% over random on its own**, i.e. almost all of the achievable lift.

So the honest decomposition of the ranker's advantage is:

> **~69% of the lift over random is meal_time/category context — which any context-aware baseline recovers — and cart-awareness specifically adds a measurable but modest ~3% on top of a context-aware baseline** (Precision@3 +3.4%, NDCG@3 +3.2%). The "~3%" is always stated as "over a context-aware baseline" — i.e. on top of meal_time already being known — never as an unqualified "3% model effect", since the model also earns the meal_time-context-modelling credit that the bare popularity number doesn't capture.

This is a finding to state plainly, not bury. It directly answers the first thing a sharp interviewer does — the arithmetic showing popularity barely beats random — and it's defensible because the generator was built that way on purpose: the within-cart ranking signal is dominated by `(meal_time, category)` affinity (constant per category), with a smaller genuinely cart-dependent term (a nonlinear `sin(price_ratio)` that depends on `cart_total_price`). The model's top-2 features by split count, `cart_total_price` and `price_ratio`, are exactly the levers of that cart-dependent term — which is consistent with the ~3% the ranker squeezes out beyond the cart-blind baseline coming from cart composition.

**Where this leaves the project claim:** the defensible claim is "a correctly-built learning-to-rank pipeline that beats a context-aware baseline by a measurable-but-modest margin on a deliberately noisy generator," **not** "cart-awareness dominates." Claiming the latter would be exactly the kind of overstatement the third baseline exists to prevent.

## Does city actually matter? (per-city sensitivity — reported as its own finding)

Two independent checks, because "city is in the feature list" is not evidence that it changes a recommendation.

**1. Feature importance.** `city_encoded` ranks **#3 of 19** by split count (1203) — the model spends real capacity on it. Good evidence on its own, but importance measures *use*, not whether use changes the *output*.

**2. City-pair probe (`model_training/city_sensitivity_check.py`).** An earlier 10-city table was weak evidence: 8 of 10 cities picked nearly the same top item, scores clustered within 0.037 — indistinguishable from close-call noise near a decision boundary. The fairer test is the city *pair* that should differ most given the hidden structure we actually set up, holding **cart, cuisine, and meal_time fixed** and varying only the city. We grounded the pair choice in the real parameters rather than guessing:

- By **`CITY_CUISINE_PRIOR`** (which shapes how many carts of each cuisine a city generates, and therefore how much the model has seen each city in each context): the most different pair is **Bangalore vs Lucknow** (L1 distance 1.00 — Bangalore skews Italian/South-Indian, Lucknow skews Mughlai/North-Indian).
- By **`HIDDEN_CITY_WEIGHT`** (the actual label-time mechanism — it scales the cuisine-affinity term in the logit): the most different pair is **Mumbai (0.549) vs Kolkata (0.224)**.

Probe setup: cart = one Mughlai Main, cuisine = Mughlai, meal_time = Dinner, 25 candidates, scored with the real trained model.

| Pair | Top-1 same? | Top-3 overlap | Spearman(rank) | Mean-score level shift |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Bangalore vs Lucknow** (cuisine-prior) | **No** — different dessert wins | 3/3 | **0.835** | −0.054 |
| Mumbai vs Kolkata (hidden-weight) | Yes | 2/3 | 0.957 | −0.028 |

**Finding:** for the most-different pair (Bangalore vs Lucknow), the model's **top recommendation actually flips** — Bangalore's top add-on is `Mughlai_Dessert_22` (0.226), Lucknow's is `Mughlai_Dessert_23` (0.120) — and the full-candidate ranking correlation drops to 0.835. That is real, visible city sensitivity, not a level shift: city changes *which* item is recommended, not just the score magnitude. Interestingly the cuisine-prior pair reorders *more* than the hidden-weight pair, which makes sense — `CITY_CUISINE_PRIOR` controls how much Mughlai-at-Dinner data the model saw for each city, so the model's city-specific behaviour is shaped as much by **differential training exposure** as by the hidden weight itself. The honest summary: **city matters to the ranking, and is demonstrable on the pair where it should matter most — but its effect is second-order to meal_time/category**, consistent with the feature-importance ordering.

## On the noise ceiling (is this circular / memorized?)

The generator adds irreducible Gaussian logit noise on purpose (see `docs/CHANGES.md`), so the achievable ceiling is well below a memorized 1.0. NDCG@3 = 0.239 is comfortably in the "real, partially-learnable signal" regime, not the "perfectly memorized lookup table" regime — exactly what a de-circularized generator should produce. The regression test `test_labels_are_not_deterministic_given_features_alone` guards this from the data side; these metrics corroborate it from the model side. If a future change pushed NDCG toward ~0.99, that would signal circularity had crept back in and should be investigated before trusting any other number.

> Historical note: before `lightgbm` was installable in the prep sandbox, the pipeline and evaluation code were validated end-to-end with a `RandomForestClassifier` stand-in (Test AUC ≈ 0.65, Precision@3 ≈ 0.225). Those were sanity-check numbers only; the table above supersedes them with the real ranker run, and the ballpark match (≈0.225 then, 0.228 now) is a useful consistency check that the evaluation harness behaves the same across both.

## What we are NOT claiming

- **No claim of real-world revenue impact.** The original pitch's "+18% revenue uplift" and "₹16 Crore annual impact" were illustrative hackathon-slide numbers, not measured results, and are not carried forward. Any projected number must be labelled as a projection and derived transparently.
- **No claim that cart-awareness dominates.** Per the three-baseline decomposition above, most of the lift over the naive popularity baseline is meal_time context; cart modelling adds a measurable but modest ~3% on top. Stated honestly rather than rounded up into the headline.
- **No claim this generalizes to real consumer behavior** without re-validating on real order data, which is explicitly out of scope for this version (see `docs/CHANGES.md`, limitations section).
